Primary Sources

  1. "Albanians Protest Serb And Greek Rule." New York Times [New York] 7 July 1921. New York Times Archives. New York Times. 1 Mar. 2009 <http://query.nytimes.com/gst/abstract.html?res=9505E0D7163EEE3ABC4F53DFB166838A639EDE&emc=eta1>.
    This article from the New York Times described an appeal to the League of Nations from Albania about its Serbian neighbor. It provided a view into the dialogue which the League facilitates and allowed us to see the day to day workings of the League of Nations.
  2. "The Covenant of the League of Nations." Avalon Project. Lillian Gold Law Library - Yale Law School. 1 Mar. 2009 <http://avalon.law.yale.edu/20th_century/leagcov.asp>.
    This is the essential document which lays the foundation of the League of Nations. Reading these articles helped us understand the inner workings of the League and its principles.
  3. "Finns And Swedes Put Aland Dispute In League's Hands." New York Times [New York] 19 Sept. 1920. New York Times Archives. New York Times. 1 Mar. 2009 <http://query.nytimes.com/gst/abstract.html?res=9F0CE7DC173AE532A2575AC1A96F9C946195D6CF&emc=eta1>.
    In this article, we were able to see the League of Nations as an arbiter. Sweden and Finland allowed the League of Nations to intervene in their land dispute of the Aland territory. Allowing the League of Nations to intervene was the strongest sign of support for the League. This article helped us understand the responsibilities of the League and other nations’ perceptions of it.
  4. Henry, Lodge Cabot. "League of Nations Must Be Revised." Washington, D.C. History Archives. W.W Norton and Company. 1 Mar. 2009 <http://www.wwnorton.com/college/history/archive/resources/documents/ch25_06.htm>.
    This speech by Senator Cabot to Congress allowed us to see the major arguments against the League and the type of opposition Wilson encountered in the United States.
  5. League of Nations Assembly - 1 September 1928, Geneva Switzerland. William Lyon Mackenzie King Collection. Canadian Navy Heritage Project: Photo Archive. National Archives of Canada. 1 Mar. 2009 <http://www.navy.gc.ca/project_pride/all_images/photo_archive_images/Large/C-033867.jpg>.
    This photo contained the delegations which comprised the League of Nations. It is truly historical in that it shows the men and women who made the League possible.
  6. League of Nations Assembly, 1932. Japan’s Pan-Asianism and the Legitimacy of Imperial World Order, 1931 & 1945. The Asia-Pacific Journal. 1 Mar. 2009 <http://www.japanfocus.org/_Cemil_Aydin-Japan___s_Pan_Asianism_and_the_Legitimacy_of_Imperial_World_Order__1931___1945/>.
    This photo of the League of Nations assembly gave us a physical representation of the structure of the League and placed us directly into that timeframe.
  7. "Lodge And Lowell Debate Covenant." New York Times [New York] 20 Mar. 1920. New York Times Archive. New York Times. 1 Mar. 2009 <http://query.nytimes.com/gst/abstract.html?res=9A0DE0DD1331E433A25753C2A9659C946896D6CF&emc=eta1>.
    This New York Times articles shows the debate which surrounded the League of Nations during its inception. Senator Lodge led the criticism, saying the draft of the Covenant of the League of Nations at the time needed key revisions in order to gain his support. This newspaper article was helpful in that it showed the process which resulted in the League of Nations today.
  8. Paderewski, Ignace. "Statement by Paderewski on the League of Nations, 18 September 1919." First World War.com. 29 May 2004. 1 Mar. 2009 <http://www.firstworldwar.com/source/poland_paderewski2.htm>.
    This statement by the Prime Minister of Poland in support of the League of Nations helped us to see how other countries saw the League as beneficial. Being aware of multiple perspectives gave us a broader understand of our subject.
  9. The Palaise Wilson, headquarters of the League of Nations Secretariat from 1920-1936 in Geneva. Historical Picture Collections. University of San Diego. 1 Mar. 2009 <http://history.sandiego.edu/gen/WW1Pics/90856a.jpg>.
    Awareness of the location of the League of Nation’s headquarters helps place our knowledge in a physical context. A visual aid adds personality to the League of Nations.
  10. "Plan To Save Austria Is Made Effective." New York Times [New York] 5 Oct. 1922. New York Times Archives. New York Times. 1 Mar. 2009 <http://query.nytimes.com/gst/abstract.html?res=9505E1DB1E39EF3ABC4D53DFB6678389639EDE&emc=eta1>.
    This article from the New York Times describes the signing of a loan to the struggling government of Austria in the 1920s. This occasion is important because the League, in saving Austria from economic collapse, was fulfilling its purpose. It showed the positive effect the League can have when it is working at its best.
  11. "United Nations Organization is Born." BBC [London] 24 Oct. 1945. BBC. 1 Mar. 2009 <http://news.bbc.co.uk/onthisday/hi/dates/stories/october/24/newsid_4353000/4353094.stm>.
    This article from the BBC marked the end of the League of Nations. The duties of the League were formally transferred to the United Nations, which continues to serve the world today. This article helped us understand the end of the League of Nations.
  12. Wilson, Woodrow. "Final Address in Support of the League of Nations." Pueblo, Colorado. American Rhetoric: Woodrow Wilson. 1 Mar. 2009 <http://www.pbs.org/greatwar/resources/casdeath_pop.html>.
    This final speech represented Wilson’s relentless effort to rally support for the League of Nations. In this speech, Wilson addressed the points which the opposition brings up in clear simple terms. He appeals to his audience to view the League of Nations as a necessary tool for world peace. This speech helped us understand Wilson’s efforts to convince the nation to accept the League of Nations.
  13. Wilson, Woodrow. "Fourteen Points." Washington, D.C. First World War.com. 21 Oct. 2001. 1 Mar. 2009 <http://www.firstworldwar.com/source/fourteenpoints.htm>.
    This famous speech by Wilson to Congress was the first time the concept of the League of Nations was introduced. This speech helped us understand the emergence of the League of Nations and its development from a “general association of nations” to a tangible body.
  14. Woodrow Wilson Picture - 28th President. Woodrow Wilson. Visitingdc.com. 1 Mar. 2009 <http://www.visitingdc.com/president/woodrow-wilson-picture.htm>.
    A picture of Woodrow Wilson was absolutely necessary on our website. Because he is the focus of our project, connecting a face to the person helped us understand that our project is about a real individual in history who lived for his cause and not just a vague historical figure.
  15. "The World's Hope Of Mercy And Of Peace." New York Times [New York] 26 Sept. 1919. New York Times Archive. New York Times. 1 Mar. 2009 <http://query.nytimes.com/gst/abstract.html?res=9A03E4DC123AE03ABC4E51DFBF668382609EDE&emc=eta1>.
    This New York Times article is a transcript of one of Wilson’s later speeches in support of the League of Nations. In this speech, Wilson described in clear simple language the advantages of the League and why it is crucial to world peace. This helped us understand Wilson’s passion for the League.

Secondary Sources

  1. Strollo, Phillip J. "League of Nations Timeline." The World at War. 1 Mar. 2009 <http://worldatwar.net/timeline/other/league18-46.html>.
    This source gave us the membership of the League of Nations over time. It helped us to see the diversity which comprised the League and also its growth as time when on. It also showed us the decline of the League membership especially as we got closer to WWII, which helped illustrate the eventual failure of the League.
  2. The Eleanor Roosevelt Papers."League of Nations." Teaching Eleanor Roosevelt, ed. by Allida Black, June Hopkins, et. al. (Hyde Park, New York: Eleanor Roosevelt National Historic Site, 2003). http://www.nps.gov/archive/elro/glossary/league-of-nations.htm [Accessed March 1, 2009].
    This website provided much of the information we had about the League of Nations. It was helpful in the writing of the content of our website.
  3. "Wilson- A Portrait: League of Nations." American Experience: Woodrow Wilson. Public Broadcasting System. 1 Mar. 2009 <http://www.pbs.org/wgbh/amex/wilson/portrait/wp_league.html>.
    This website gave background information on Wilson and the League of Nations. It was a good starting point for our research.
  4. "Woodrow Wilson: Foreign Affairs." American President: An Online Reference Source. Miller Center of Public Affairs - University of Virginia. 1 Mar. 2009 <http://millercenter.org/academic/americanpresident/wilson/essays/biography/5>.
    This website gave a detailed account of Wilson’s foreign policy during his presidency. This was where we learned about Wilson’s collapse because of his tireless efforts to gather support for the League of Nations.
  5. "WWI Casualty and Deaths Tables." The Great War. Public Broadcasting System. 1 Mar. 2009 <http://www.pbs.org/greatwar/resources/casdeath_pop.html>.
    This provided information for the introductory paragraph of our website. It helped us understand the impact of World War I.
