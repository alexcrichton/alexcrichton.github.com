A New World Order

Despite Wilson’s relentless efforts, the United States never did ratify the Treaty of Versailles until 1921, when it was amended for the US to opt out of the League of Nations. Wilson fervently believed the Covenant must not be altered for the League to have any effect when subduing a nation’s “passions” and preventing future wars. He would not stand for a compromise. When Senator Lodge amended the treaty, Wilson instructed his supporters to vote against it and it failed.

While the US never became a full member of the League of Nations, there were numerous states that did. The list of members ranged from Canada to China to Venezuela and Japan. In accordance to Wilson’s vision, the League of Nations provided a forum for nations to voice their opinions as equals and resolve disputes without resorting to force. Between 1920 and 1925, the League was able to resolve a border dispute between Finland and Sweden – skirting a possible war. In 1922, the League saved Austria from economic collapse. Smaller nations, such as Albania, saw the League as a means to voice their grievances without fear of retaliation. However, the League had its weaknesses. It lacked the power to enforce controversial decisions and it was further handicapped by the failure of the United States to join. Nazi Germany withdrew from the League in 1933. With the onset of World War II, the League had become marginalized to the point where any meaningful diplomacy only conducted at the bilateral level. It effectively ceased to function during and after World War II until it was formally dissolved by the formation of the Charter of the United Nations.

While the League of Nations ultimately failed, it was an invaluable experiment in the realm of international relations. It opened the door for a new genre of diplomacy that could be used to bring the world closer to peace. The League of Nations, with both its strengths and failures, provided a model for the consequent United Nations. The United Nations which plays an integral peacekeeping role in the world today was made stronger because it avoided the mistakes of the League of Nations. Without the perseverance of President Woodrow Wilson, the concept of a united body of all nations would not be common knowledge today, and the world would have been one step further from peace.
