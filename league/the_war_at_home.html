The War at Home

Woodrow Wilson worked hard to push the League of Nations and the rest of the Fourteen Points to the top of the agenda at Versailles. He was not able to incorporate all of his points into the treaty but was able to push the League of Nations to the forefront of the final draft of the Treaty of Versailles. The first 26 of the treaty’s 440 articles formed the Covenant of the League.

The crux of the Covenant was Article 10 which obligates member states to “respect and preserve as against external aggression the territorial integrity and existing political independence of all Members of the League.” Article 10 and Article 11 encompassed an international collective security. This naturally gave rise to as much opposition as support from the countries involved. Smaller states advocated the Covenant, recognizing the benefits of a mutual alliance with world powers. Polish Prime Minister Ignace Paderewski stated:

Unless we have a protective power in the world, under whose strength we can secure an opportunity for peaceful development and the solution of our internal problems, free from distracting and antagonistic influences, I fear for the safety of our democracy.
The great power and the support which it may furnish need not be military, its moral and economic force is all that we ask, and that power is the League of Nations.
Others saw the League in a different. In the United States, Wilson faced significant opposition led by Senator Henry Cabot Lodge. Senator Lodge was a staunch Republican who believed that Article 10 of the covenant amounted to a cession of sovereignty from the United States, declaring:
We have been devoted to the cause of freedom, humanity, and civilization everywhere. Now we are asked, in the making of peace, to sacrifice our sovereignty in important respects, to involve ourselves almost without limit in the affairs of other nations and to yield up policies and rights which we have maintained throughout our history.
He interpreted the clause as a possible requirement for the United States to commit economic or military force in order to preserve the collective security of the member states. He viewed as an obligation as intolerable:
In what I have already said about other nations putting us into war I have covered one point of sovereignty which ought never to be yielded—the power to send American soldiers and sailors everywhere, which ought never to be taken from the American people or impaired in the slightest degree. Let us beware how we palter with our independence. We have not reached the great position from which we were able to come down into the field of battle and help to save the world from tyranny by being guided by others. Our vast power has all been built up and gathered together by ourselves alone.

Having authored Article 10, Wilson vehemently defended the League and the rest of the Covenant. He maintained that the United States had the ability to veto any resolution from the League which it objected. Realizing that the League of Nations was a key point of contention for the Senate ratification of the Treaty of Versailles, Wilson embarked upon a tour of the nation to garner support for the Covenant. Traveling 9,981, Wilson gave speeches in 29 cities for his dream of a new international order. In his last speech at Pueblo, Colorado on September 25th, 1919, Wilson affirmed the League of Nations as crucial step towards world peace:

You will say, “Is the League and absolute guaranty against war?” No; I do not know any absolute guaranty against the errors of human judgment or the violence of human passion…I ask you this: If it is not an absolute insurance against war, do you want no insurance at all? Do you want nothing? Do you want not only no probability that war will not recur, but the probability that it will recur? The arrangements of justice of not stand of themselves, my fellow citizens. The arrangements of this treaty are just, but they need the support of the combined power of the great nations of the world.
Shortly after delivering these words to the crowd at Pueblo, Colorado, President Wilson collapsed. He had worked himself to exhaustion while fighting for his cause. Disregarding his own health, Wilson refused to stand by and allow the atrocities of WWI to recur. He later suffered a stroke that paralyzed half of his body and isolated him for the rest of his presidency.
